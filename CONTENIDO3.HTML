THE WEHRMACHT

Wehrmacht is the German word for Defense Force, from "wehren" (to defend) and "Macht" (strength). Therefore, in German, the term applies to any country and not only to the German armed forces, although the term has been widely used in publications in other languages to refer to the German armed forces of the period 1935-1945. Thus, in German, for example, "italienische wehrmacht" means the Italian Fierce Armed Forces. After World War II the Armed Forces of the Federal Republic of Germany are called Bundeswehr, literally "Federal Defense".

At the end of the First World War, on November 11, 1919, the German armed forces were called "Friedensheer" or Peace Force and after the institution of the Weimar Republic, on March 6, 1919, a decree was issued creating the "Vorläufige Reichswehr" or Provisional Defense Force of the Reich which was formed with just over 400,000 troops. However, the Treaty of Versailles of May 1919, limited the power of the Vorläufige Reichswehr to a maximum of 100,000 troops, of which only 4,000 could be officers, without the existence of a General Staff and the prohibition of military conscription.

Between 1933 and the end of 1936 the Deutsches Wehrmacht went from 100,000 to 550,000 men in arms, and by 1939 the number of all weapons amounted to 2,750,000 men. When the restructuring of the Heeres (Army) began in 1935, it became a fast moving mobile machine equipped with modern rolling stock intercommunicated by radio and supported by the Luftwaffe.

Deutsches Wehrmacht

In 1933, immediately after National Socialism took power and after Adolf Hitler was recognized as the new Chancellor, the Treaty of Versailles was denounced. The military immediately supported the resolution signed by Hitler with his new foreign policy, which evidently aimed at revising the structure of the Reichswehr.

To accelerate the process of modernization and expansion of the armed forces, the Reichwehr was replaced by the new Wehrmacht, unified armed forces that included the Heeres (Army), the Kriegsmarine (Navy) and the Luftwaffe (Air Force).

The Kriegsmarine was also modernized starting with the launch of the battleship Scharnhorst in 1936, followed by the Gneisenaau in 1938 and a year later with the Bismarck and Tirpitz, in accordance with Plan Z, which was never completed. In 1939 the Kriegsmarine had 50,000 troops, three battleships, two heavy cruisers and six light cruisers, 22 destroyers, 16 torpedo boats and 57 submarines, an insufficient force to face the Royal Navy, so the construction of submarines increased extraordinarily. On March 1st 1935, the Luftwaffe joined the Wehrmacht as the third weapon with 400,000 men and more than 4,000 planes of the latest design. It was in its time a fearsome force with unparalleled air superiority.

In 1939, wrongly and against the opinion of his generals, Hitler considered the Wehrmacht ready to support his plans to recover the territories lost by the Treaty of Versailles and before Poland took the initiative, which was seen coming, he began the offensive that in five weeks destroyed the largest army in Europe, although poorly equipped and trained with World War I tactics.
